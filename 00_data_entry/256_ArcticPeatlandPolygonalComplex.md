## Overview

**Unit Name:** Arctic Peatland Polygonal Complex

**Level:** Map Class

**Code:** 256

**Concept:** This [level] encompasses polygonal tundra complexes of minerotrophic or ombrotrophic peatlands that are dominated by hydrophytic sedges and mosses, often in combination with dwarf and prostrate shrubs. Wetland sedges, including *Carex aquatilis* and *Eriophorum angustifolium* and wet-site mosses including members of the *Sphagnum*, *Scorpidium*, *Calliergion*, and *Drepanocladus* genera occur in lower microsites such as polygon troughs and low centers, dwarf and prostrate shrubs including *Salix pulchra*, *Rhododendron tomentosum*, and *Vaccinium vitis-idaea* occur on higher microsites such as polygon ridges and high centers; tussocks are not present. The type develops across flat terrain underlain by near-surface and ice-rich permafrost. While not restricted to coastal physiologies, the complexes are common across the outer margin of the Arctic Coastal Plain.

### Floristics

**Floristics Description:** This [level] is indicated by wetland sedges, such as *Carex aquatilis* and *Eriophorum* species, and hydrophytic mosses including species of *Sphagnum*, *Scorpidium*, *Calliergion*, and *Drepanocladus*, often occupying moist to wet troughs of high-centered polygons and the surfaces of low-centered polygons. Low willows, such as *Salix pulchra* and dwarf ericaceous shrubs, such as *Rhododendron tomentosum* and *Vaccinium vitis-idaea*, often occupy moist microhighs in complex with these peatlands. The [unit_name] combines floristic elements of mineotrophic and ombrotrophic shrub-sedge, Sphagnum-sedge, and brown moss-sedge peatland alliances; please refer to these descriptions for further detail.

## Dynamics

**Dynamics Description:** Polygonal complexes develop across relatively flat terrain underlain by shallow and ice-rich permafrost where seasonal thawing and refreezing of surface soils, also known as the active layer, causes coincident cycles of expansion and contraction. Repetition of these cycles over decades forms a network of cracks separating intact, often polygonal portions of tundra. As meltwater repeatedly migrates to the cracks and refreezes, a network of ice wedges forms. Continued ice wedge aggradation and the associated displacement of overlying soil can raise the elevation of polygon rims relative to their centers, forming low-center polygons. The transition to high-center polygons occurs through a combination of organic matter accumulation, in the center, and thermal erosion of ice wedges and collapse of polygon rims at the margins. Any disturbance that changes the hydrothermal regime of these systems threatens their existence. Where climate change results in deeper seasonal thaw, interstitial and massive soil ice may degrade, causing thermokarst and cryoturbation. Inundation resulting from thaw thus provides a positive feedback to the further degradation of permafrost.

## Environment

**Environment Description**: The [unit_name] requires the presence of ice-rich permafrost and low-lying terrain such as a basin or coastal plains for development. Complexes frequently occur in drained lake beds and low-lying depressions with poor drainage. Polygonal terrain includes low, wet to moist troughs, mesic ice wedge rims, wet, low polygon centers, and mesic high polygon centers. As a peatland, soils are wet (hygric to hydric) and predominantly organic, however cryoturbation may bring mix mineral soils with overlying organic horizons, and in extreme cases, bring them to the surface as frost boils. Physiography and water chemistry distinguish minerotrophic from ombrotrophic wetlands, resulting in differences in species composition, productivity, and soil properties. Systems may be fed by precipitation (ombrotrophic) and acidic, or fed by groundwater (mineotrophic) with circumneutral pH.
