## Overview

**Unit Name:** Arctic Shrub-Sedge Peatland, Minerotrophic

**Level:** Map Class

**Code:** 264

**Concept:** This [level] encompasses minerotrophic peatlands underlain by near-surface permafrost, dominated or co-dominated by combinations of dwarf to low shrubs, hydrophytic sedges and brown mosses (i.e., non-*Sphagnum* mosses). Soils are saturated, circumneutral, and cold causing moderate-nutrient and moderate-productivity conditions. This type is susceptible to change in hydrothermal regime.

### Floristics

**Floristics Description:** This [level] is dominated by *Salix richardsonii* in the low shrub layer; *Eriophorum angustifolium* and *Carex aquatilis* are the most common sedges that co-occur. Other common plants include dwarf shrubs such as *Salix reticulata, Dryas integrifolia,* and *Arctous rubra*, and herbs such as *Polygonum viviparum, Saxifraga hirculus,* and *Equisetum variegatum*. The most frequently occurring non-*Sphagnum* (i.e., brown) mosses are *Tomentypnum nitens, Campylium stellatum, Limprichtia revolvens, Hamatocaulis vernicosus, Calliergon richardsonii,* and *Aulacomnium palustre*. Common lichen species include *Dactylina arctica* and *Flavocetraria cucullata.*

## Dynamics

**Dynamics Description:** Dominance of brown mosses represents an early stage of peat formation with comparatively thin accumulations of organic material. Where regional increase in temperature results in permafrost degradation and improved drainage, sites may transition out of wetland conditions. Conversely, retarded drainage may transition sites to aquatic habitat or where flow is decreased, a more ombrotrophic wetland.

## Range

**Range Description:** Occurring across the Arctic bioclimatic zone, broadly associated with the Arcitc Coastal Plain.

**Rarity on Landscape:** Common

## Environment

**Environment Description**: This [level] is a type of minerotrophic wetland that forms on upper floodplain positions and drained lake basins. Minerotrophic wetlands receive water from ground or surface water that is rich in dissolved minerals. Physiography and water chemistry distinguish minerotrophic from ombrotrophic wetland types, leading to predictable differences in species composition, productivity, and soil properties. The regular, seasonal flooding and sedimentation on upper floodplains create a more productive environment than lowlands, which are often nutrient-limited.
