## Overview

**Unit Name:** Arctic Non-tussock Polygonal Complex

**Level:** Map Class

**Code:** 252

**Concept:** This [level] encompasses polygonal tundra complexes underlain by near-surface and relatively ice-rich permafrost that are dominated or co-dominated by combinations of the mesic sedges *Carex bigelowii* and *Carex scirpoidea* and the prostrate shrub *Dryas integrifolia*. Dwarf *Salix* shrubs, bryophytes, and lichens are common and abundant. Soils are sandy and moderately well drained, leading to a comparatively muted development of polygons where centers are flat to raised and troughs are shallow without standing water. Tussocks are not present. The type is common on the Arctic Coastal Plain where it is associated with loess deposited from large river systems.

### Floristics

**Floristics Description:** This [level] is indicated by mesic graminoids occurring in complexes with prostrate- and/or dwarf-shrub tundra. Common sedges are *Carex bigelowii*, *Carex scirpoidea*, and in wetter microsites, *Carex aquatilis* and *Eriophorum angustifolium*; accessory grasses may include *Arctagrostis latifolia* and *Poa arctica*.  Dwarf shrub communities are commonly dominated by *Dryas integrifolia* and willows such as *Salix arctica*, *Salix reticulata*, but may include minor contributions of ericaceous shrubs such as *Vaccinium vitis-idaea*. Accessory forbs may include *Bistorta vivipara*, *Bistorta plumosa*, *Saxifraga hirculus*, and *Pedicularis capitata*. Common lichens are *Flavocetraria cucullata*, *Flavocetraria nivalis*, *Thamnolia vermicularis*, *Dactylina arctica*, *Masonhalea richardsonii*, and *Ochrolechia frigida*. *Racomitrium lanuginosum* and *Polytrichum juniperinum* are common mosses. The [unit_name] combines floristic elements of the Arctic Dryas (-Willow) Dwarf Shrub and Arctic Herbaceous Nontussock Tundra alliances; please refer to these descriptions for further detail.

## Dynamics

**Dynamics Description:** Polygonal complexes develop across relatively flat terrain underlain by shallow and ice-rich permafrost where seasonal thawing and refreezing of surface soils, also known as the active layer, causes coincident cycles of expansion and contraction. Repetition of these cycles over decades forms a network of cracks separating intact, often polygonal portions of tundra. As meltwater repeatedly migrates to the cracks and refreezes, a network of ice wedges forms. Continued ice wedge aggradation and the associated displacement of overlying soil can raise the elevation of polygon rims relative to their centers, forming low-center polygons. The transition to high-center polygons occurs through a combination of organic matter accumulation, in the center, and thermal erosion of ice wedges and collapse of polygon rims at the margins. Any disturbance that changes the hydrothermal regime of these systems threatens their existence. Where climate change results in deeper seasonal thaw, interstitial and massive soil ice may degrade, causing thermokarst and cryoturbation. Inundation resulting from thaw thus provides a positive feedback to the further degradation of permafrost.

## Range

**Range Description:** Occurring across the Arctic bioclimatic zone, largely restricted to sandy soils on the Arctic Coastal Plain.

**Rarity on Landscape:** Common

## Environment

**Environment Description:** This [level] forms over relatively ice-rich permafrost that undergoes seasonal freeze-thaw cycles, causing the ground to contract and expand and resulting in a reticulate network of ice wedges. Nontussock polygonal tundra complexes develop across low-lying terrain that is better drained than tussock-forming and peatland polygonal complexes. Soils are sandy with coarse fragments and thin surface organics. Loess blown from large river systems contributes to circumneutral to alkaline soils. The greater proportion of mineral material promotes drainage and leads to a comparatively muted development of polygonal tundra. Different from peatland and tussock tundra polygon complexes, nontussock polygonal complexes are often smaller diameter with flat to raised centers and shallow troughs that lack surface water.
