### Floristics

**Floristics Description:** This [level] is indicated by the willow, *Salix alaxensis* with other willow species, namely *Salix richardsonii, *Salix niphoclada*, and *Salix glauca* codominating. Diagnostic herbaceous plants include the forbs *Eurybia sibirica*, *Artemisia tilesii*, *Parnassia kotzebuei*, *Tanacetum bipinnatum* ssp. *bipinnatum*, and *Chamerion latifolium*, the horsetails *Equisetum arvense* and *Equisetum variegatum*, and the grass *Festuca rubra*. On substrates that experience infrequent flooding, dwarf and prostrate shrubs including *Salix reticulata*, *Dryas integrifolia,* and *Arctous rubra* may establish in the understory. Bryophytes include *Sanionia uncinata, Brachythecium mildeanum, Leptobryum pyriforme, Campylium stellatum*, and *Bryum pseudotriquetrum*; dry site lichens, such as those in the *Stereocaulon genus,* may be present at low abundance.

## Dynamics

**Dynamics Description:** The [unit_name] type experiences regular flooding or saturation during the growing season. Across floodplains in the Arctic, both the energy of flooding and depth of seasonal thaw decreases away from the main channel. These gradients are paralleled by decreases in sediment grain size, drainage, and shrub stature and increase in understory development. In the active floodplain, high energy flooding may scour vegetation and erode and redeposit sediment. In the inactive floodplain, lower energy and less frequent flooding is often accompanied by the deposition of fine-grained sediment. Permafrost is present at depth but is not a formative element. As this type is maintained by hydrology, any change to the frequency of flooding and duration of inundation or saturation will have commensurate effects on vegetation. For example, as the main channel migrates across the valley floor, sites can become removed from the active or inactive floodplain, eventually transitioning to upland tundra.

## Environment

**Environment Description**: This [level] forms linear bands across low gradient riverine floodplains that are subject to regular flooding or saturation. Owing to the high capacity of water to retain heat, the depth to permafrost shallows with distance from the main channel. As the energy and frequency of flooding also decreases with distance from the main channel, soils tend to be coarse grained and well drained in the active floodplain and finer grained and more moderately drained in the inactive floodplain. Greater rooting depth and drainage facilitates the establishment and growth of shrubs in the active floodplain, yet the erosion and deposition of sediment accompanying frequent and high energy flooding limits development of the herbaceous understory. Conversely in the inactive floodplain, shallower rooting depth limits the height of shrubs, yet less frequent and lower energy flooding that often delivers nutrient-rich silts and fine sands, promotes the growth of a species-rich and semi-continuous understory.
